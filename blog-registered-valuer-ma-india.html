The Role of a Registered Valuer in Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A) in India

Published on May 29, 2026
7 min read

Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A) are among the most complex strategic maneuvers a company can undertake. Beyond the intense negotiations, cultural integration, and legal due diligence, the absolute core of any M&A deal is the valuation. In India, the law mandates that this critical task be overseen by an IBBI Registered Valuer. Their role is not merely mathematical; it is a legal safeguard to protect the interests of shareholders, creditors, and regulatory bodies.

1. Determining the Swap Ratio

In a merger or amalgamation, the target company's shareholders receive shares in the acquiring company. The ratio at which these shares are exchanged is known as the "Swap Ratio" or "Share Exchange Ratio."

Calculating the swap ratio requires valuing both the target company and the acquiring company simultaneously. An IBBI Registered Valuer provides an independent, objective valuation report determining a fair swap ratio, ensuring minority shareholders are not disadvantaged by a skewed deal structure.

2. Regulatory Compliance (Section 230-232)

Under Sections 230 to 232 of the Companies Act, 2013, any scheme of compromise, arrangement, or amalgamation must be approved by the National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT). A mandatory requirement for this approval is a valuation report prepared by a Registered Valuer.

The NCLT relies heavily on the Registered Valuer's report to ensure the transaction is fair and equitable before sanctioning the scheme.

"In an M&A transaction, the Registered Valuer acts as the independent anchor of truth, balancing the aggressive projections of the seller with the conservative estimates of the buyer."

3. Purchase Price Allocation (PPA)

Post-acquisition, Indian Accounting Standards (Ind AS 103) require the acquiring company to allocate the purchase price across the acquired assets and liabilities at their fair values. This often involves valuing intangible assets that don't appear on the balance sheet, such as:

  • Brand value and trademarks
  • Customer relationships and contracts
  • Patents and proprietary technology

The remaining unallocated premium is recorded as Goodwill. A Registered Valuer specializes in these complex intangible asset valuations.

4. Tax Implications and FEMA

If the M&A involves cross-border transactions (e.g., an Indian company acquiring a foreign entity or vice versa), the valuation must comply with the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) pricing guidelines. Furthermore, the Income Tax Act scrutinizes M&A deals to prevent tax evasion through under-valued asset transfers. A comprehensive valuation report serves as the primary defense during tax assessments.

Conclusion

The role of a Registered Valuer in M&A goes far beyond crunching numbers in a spreadsheet. They are the regulatory linchpin that ensures a transaction is fair, legally compliant, and structured correctly for tax and accounting purposes. Engaging an experienced valuer early in the M&A process is critical for a smooth and successful deal closure.
